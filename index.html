Driving deaths down

The number of people dying on America roadways has been in steady decline for more than a decade.

By Adrian Jania

The number of people dying as a result of car accidents has declined steadily from 2001 to 2014, according to the Centers for Disease Control and Preventionâ€™s motor vehicle death rate data.

With vehicle safety improving and the number of traffic laws increasing, it is no surprise that the numbers have gone down over the years.

In the 13 year span, Wyoming saw the greatest decrease in fatalities with 11.9 per 100,000 deaths less in 2014 than in 2001. It could have been lower: from 2013 to 2014 Wyoming went from 10.8 per 100,000 deaths to 11.9.

In the US, the overall fatality rate has decreased in every state exceot North Dakota. The state experienced a peak in motor vehicle fatalities in 2012 with 24.3 per 100,000 deaths.

Aside from North Dakota, the final year of collected data appears optimistic.

According to the Insurance Institute for Highway Safety, the rate of crash deaths per 100,000 population has declined by more than half since they began collecting data in 1975.

Contributing to the death toll are alcohol, speeding, lack of safety belt use, and other problematic driver behaviors, according to IIHS.
